What is multiplexing?

Multiplexing allows multiple signals to share a single physical channel/medium simultaneously. A multiplexer (MUX) combines signals at the sender; a demultiplexer (DEMUX) separates them at the receiver.

FDM assigns frequency bands | TDM assigns time slots | WDM assigns wavelengths (optical)

FDM — Frequency Division Multiplexing

Analog signals
Divides the available bandwidth into non-overlapping frequency bands. Each channel gets a dedicated band. Guard bands separate channels to prevent interference.

Key formula: Total BW = n × (signal BW) + (n−1) × guard band BW

Data rate: Each channel operates at its own rate — not shared.
Synchronization: Not needed — channels are always active.
Cost: High — requires bandpass filters and carrier modulation.
Real-world use: Radio broadcasting, TV, telephone (old PSTN), cable TV.

TDM — Time Division Multiplexing

Digital signals
Divides time into slots. Each channel gets a slot in a repeating frame. Entire bandwidth is available to each channel — but only during its time slot.

Two types:
— Synchronous TDM: Fixed slots per channel, even if channel has no data (wasteful). Simple, requires synchronization.
— Statistical (Asynchronous) TDM: Slots assigned only when channel has data. More efficient, needs addressing overhead.

Data rate: Link rate = sum of all channel rates (sync TDM).
Synchronization: Required — frame sync bits used.
Cost: Lower than FDM — digital, no filters needed.
Real-world use: ISDN, T1/E1 telephone lines, SONET.

WDM — Wavelength Division Multiplexing

Optical fiber — light wavelengths
Conceptually identical to FDM but applied to optical fiber. Different colors (wavelengths) of light carry different signals on the same fiber.

DWDM (Dense WDM): Packs 160+ channels on one fiber. Used in backbone internet.
Data rate: Extremely high — each wavelength can carry 10+ Gbps.
Cost: Very high — requires optical amplifiers (EDFA), precise lasers.
Advantage over FDM: No electrical conversion — photons travel at c.

What is modulation?

Modulation maps digital data (bits) onto an analog carrier wave by varying one of its properties. The three properties of a sine wave — amplitude, frequency, phase — give us ASK, FSK, and PSK.

Bit stream: 1 0 1 1 0 — Each scheme modulates a different property

ASK — Amplitude Shift Keying

Varies the amplitude (height) of the carrier. Bit 1 = high amplitude, Bit 0 = low/zero amplitude.

Bandwidth: B = (1 + d) × S, where S = signal rate (baud), d = 0 for ideal. B = S (minimum). Bandwidth is proportional to baud rate.
Noise susceptibility: Very HIGH — amplitude is the most susceptible to noise and attenuation. Noise directly corrupts amplitude.
Use: Optical fiber (simple on/off), RFID. Not used on noisy channels.

FSK — Frequency Shift Keying

Varies the frequency of the carrier. Bit 1 = higher frequency (f₁), Bit 0 = lower frequency (f₀).

Bandwidth: B = (1 + d) × S + 2Δf, where 2Δf is the frequency deviation between f₁ and f₀.
B = f₁ − f₀ + 2 × signal rate (approximately).
Noise susceptibility: MEDIUM — frequency is less affected by noise than amplitude. Better than ASK.
Use: Caller ID, radio modems, early dial-up modems (Bell 103).

PSK — Phase Shift Keying

Varies the phase of the carrier. Bit 1 = 0° phase, Bit 0 = 180° phase shift.

BPSK (Binary): 2 phases → 1 bit per symbol.
QPSK (Quadrature): 4 phases (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°) → 2 bits per symbol → double data rate for same bandwidth.

Bandwidth: Same as ASK minimum: B = S. But QPSK carries 2 bits/symbol so effective data rate = 2 × baud rate.
Noise susceptibility: LOW — phase is harder to corrupt than amplitude or frequency. Most robust.
Use: Wi-Fi (802.11), 4G LTE, satellite, Bluetooth.

QAM — Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (bonus)

Combines ASK + PSK — varies both amplitude AND phase simultaneously. Allows many bits per symbol.
QAM-16: 4 amplitude levels × 4 phase levels = 16 combinations = 4 bits/symbol.
QAM-64: 64 combinations = 6 bits/symbol. Used in cable TV (DOCSIS) and 4G/5G.

FDM Bandwidth Calculation

This exact type of numerical appeared in Batch-22 Q3 and Mids Q4. Know the FDM formula cold.

Worked example — Batch-22 Mids Q4(b) style

Problem: A voice network with 20 source-destination pairs uses FDM to exchange voice data. A voice channel has bandwidth 5 kHz. Guard band = 300 Hz. Find the total bandwidth required.


1

Identify values: n = 20 channels, channel BW = 5 kHz = 5000 Hz, guard band = 300 Hz

2

Formula: Total BW = n × (channel BW) + (n − 1) × guard band BW

3

Substitute: Total BW = 20 × 5000 + (20 − 1) × 300

4

Calculate: = 100,000 + 19 × 300 = 100,000 + 5,700

5

Answer: Total BW = 105,700 Hz = 105.7 kHz

TDM Frame Rate Calculation

Worked example — TDM link rate

Problem: 4 channels, each sending at 100 kbps, are multiplexed using synchronous TDM. Each frame has 1 bit per channel. What is the link (frame) rate?


1

Frame size: 1 bit × 4 channels = 4 bits per frame

2

Link rate: = sum of all channel rates = 4 × 100 kbps = 400 kbps

3

Frame rate: = link rate / frame size = 400,000 / 4 = 100,000 frames/sec

General formula: Link rate = n × individual channel rate (for synchronous TDM with equal rates)

ASK / FSK / PSK Bandwidth

Worked example — Mids Q4(a) style

Problem: Data rate = 1 Mbps. Find minimum bandwidth for ASK, FSK (Δf=200 kHz), BPSK, QPSK.


1

Baud rate (S): For binary (1 bit/symbol): S = 1 Mbps ÷ 1 = 1 Mbaud

2

ASK BW: B = S = 1 MHz

3

FSK BW: B = 2Δf + S = 2(200,000) + 1,000,000 = 1.4 MHz

4

BPSK BW: B = S = 1 MHz (same as ASK)

5

QPSK BW: Baud rate = 1 Mbps ÷ 2 bits/symbol = 0.5 Mbaud → B = 0.5 MHz (QPSK is twice as efficient!)

Q3(a) — Model answer: multiplexing (5 marks)

Exam answer framework

Define multiplexing (1 mark): Multiplexing is a technique that allows multiple signals from different sources to be transmitted simultaneously over a single shared communication channel. A MUX combines signals at the sender; a DEMUX separates them at the receiver.


FDM (1.5 marks): Divides available bandwidth into non-overlapping frequency bands. Each channel gets a dedicated band separated by guard bands to prevent interference. Analog signals. No synchronization needed. Total BW = n×(channel BW) + (n−1)×guard band. Used in radio, TV, cable.


TDM (1.5 marks): Divides time into slots organized in repeating frames. Each channel gets a slot in every frame. Digital signals. Synchronization required. Link rate = sum of all channel rates. Two types: synchronous (fixed slots) and statistical/asynchronous (dynamic allocation). Used in ISDN, T1, telephony.


WDM (1 mark): Uses different wavelengths of light on optical fiber — conceptually identical to FDM but for photons. DWDM packs 160+ channels. Extremely high data rate. Used in internet backbone fiber.


Comparison (examiners love this): FDM — analog, no sync, guard bands, medium cost. TDM — digital, sync needed, no guard bands, low cost. WDM — optical only, highest capacity.

Q3(b) — Model answer: modulation (5 marks)

Exam answer framework

Define modulation (0.5 mark): Modulation encodes digital data onto an analog carrier wave by varying one of its properties (amplitude, frequency, or phase).


ASK (1 mark): Varies amplitude. Bit 1 = high, Bit 0 = low/zero. BW = S (baud rate). Highest noise susceptibility — amplitude is most affected by channel noise and attenuation.


FSK (1.5 marks): Varies frequency. Bit 1 = f₁, Bit 0 = f₀. BW = f₁−f₀ + 2S (wider than ASK). Medium noise susceptibility — frequency is more stable than amplitude but requires more bandwidth.


PSK (1.5 marks): Varies phase. BPSK: 2 phases, 1 bit/symbol, BW = S. QPSK: 4 phases, 2 bits/symbol, BW = S/2 (half the bandwidth for same data rate). Lowest noise susceptibility — phase is hardest to corrupt. Most spectrally efficient.


If asked to draw waveforms: Draw a sine wave carrier, then show the transitions at each bit boundary. ASK: amplitude drops for 0. FSK: wavelength shortens for 1. PSK: waveform inverts (180° shift) for 0.
