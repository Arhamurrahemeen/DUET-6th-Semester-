Topic 1

Writing basics

The ideas that sit under every topic above: how a piece of writing is built, the qualities of good writing, and the main types.

The universal structure

Introduction
State purpose, give context
›
Body
Develop ideas with facts
›
Conclusion
Summarize, state action

Direct vs indirect order

  • Direct order: main point first, then details. Use for good or neutral news.
  • Indirect order: reasons first, main point later. Use for bad news or persuasion.

The 7 Cs of good writing common question

Clear — easy to understand
Concise — no extra words
Concrete — specific facts
Correct — accurate grammar & facts
Coherent — flows logically
Complete — nothing missing
Courteous — polite and reader-focused

Topic 2

Memo & Email

A memo is an internal message inside one organization. An email is faster and can go to many people at once. Both must be clear and get to the point.

The memo format — memorize this

Standard heading
DATE: June 4, 2026 TO: Ray Charles, Marketing Manager FROM: Bruce King, Events Manager BK SUBJECT: Conference Planning
  • Cue words (DATE, TO, FROM, SUBJECT) line up neatly
  • Use the full date: Month Day, Year
  • Add the person's job title on TO and FROM lines
  • The sender writes their initials next to their name
  • Subject is a short headline, not a full sentence
  • No "Yours sincerely" needed — memos have no closing
  • Write Encs: if you attach any documents

The three-paragraph flow

1 · Introduction
State the purpose. Who, what, where, when. "I am writing to inform…"
›
2 · Discussion
Give the facts and details. One theme per paragraph.
›
3 · Conclusion
What action to take, any deadline, end on a polite note.

Model answer — a positive memo

Scenario: You work in HR. Write a memo announcing a new Employee Wellness Program (fitness challenges, free counseling, flexible hours).

DATE: June 4, 2026 TO: All Employees FROM: A. Rajput, HR Officer AR SUBJECT: Launch of Employee Wellness Program This memo is to inform all employees that our new Employee Wellness Program will begin on April 1. The program aims to improve well-being and productivity at work. It offers three benefits: monthly fitness challenges with prizes, free mental health counseling sessions, and a flexible work schedule option. More details will be shared at an upcoming town hall. Please contact HR if you have any questions. We thank you for taking part in this effort to make our workplace healthier. Encs: Wellness Program brochure

Notice: purpose in the first line, benefits grouped in the middle, polite action close at the end.

Email — the key rules

  • Reply within a day or two; set an out-of-office if away longer
  • Subject line states the exact purpose (use FOR ACTION / FYI / URGENT only when needed)
  • Put the most important point first
  • Use bullet or numbered lists for items that should stand out
  • Short paragraphs with a space between them
  • Never write in ALL CAPS (shouting) or all lowercase (careless)
  • Always explain any attachment in the body

Model answer — a professional email

Scenario: Request an assignment extension from your professor.

Subject: Request for Extension — Data Structures Assignment Dear Professor Khan, I am writing to request a short extension on the Data Structures assignment due on June 6. I was unwell for the past three days and could not finish the work on time. I have completed most of it and only need two more days to test and submit. May I please submit it by June 8? I would be grateful for your consideration and will make sure the work is submitted by the new date. Thank you for your understanding. Best regards, Muhammad Arham Rajput Student ID: 23-CSE-13
💡 Tips & tricks
  • If a question gives you jumbled details, your job is to sort them into intro / discussion / conclusion — don't add new information
  • The first sentence must state the purpose. Examiners look for this
  • "Positive memo" means good or neutral news — use direct order (point first)
  • Memo = no greeting and no sign-off. Email = greeting and sign-off both needed

Practice questions

Q1 List the four cue words in a memo heading and what goes after each.
DATE — full calendar date (June 4, 2026) TO — recipient's name and job title FROM — sender's name, title, and initials SUBJECT — a short one-line headline of the purpose
Q2 Give three situations where you should NOT use email.
1. When personal contact is the only way to clear a misunderstanding. 2. When a phone call would be faster and more efficient. 3. When there is a real chance of miscommunication.
Q3 Write a memo reminding staff to keep the lunchroom clean after a pest problem. (try it yourself, then compare)
DATE: June 4, 2026 TO: All Staff FROM: A. Rajput, Office Manager AR SUBJECT: Keeping the Lunchroom Clean This memo is to remind all staff to clean up after using the lunchroom. We recently had a problem with insects, and the building has now been treated. To stop the problem from coming back, please wash your dishes, wipe the tables, and throw away any food waste before you leave. Leaving food out attracts pests. Your cooperation will keep our lunchroom safe and pleasant for everyone. Thank you for your help.
Topic 3

Persuasive messages

A persuasive message is used to propose an idea, request an action, or convince someone to agree. The skill is choosing the right strategy for the situation.

First, analyze the situation

  • What do you want the reader to do?
  • What objections might they have?
  • How strong is your case?
  • Which strategy fits the situation and the organization's culture?

The three strategies — which one to pick

How much resistance do you expect from the reader?
Little / none — they'll likely say yes
Direct request
Some — they may object or hesitate
Problem-solving
High — you must sell the idea
Sales pattern
  • Direct request: state the request → give details → ask for the action. e.g. requesting missed lecture slides.
  • Problem-solving: describe a shared problem → build common ground → give your solution → show benefits → ask for action. e.g. asking for a deadline extension.
  • Sales pattern (AIDA): grab attention → build interest → create desire → call for action. e.g. requesting a free software trial.

The AIDA funnel — for the sales pattern

AttentionOpen with something that grabs interest
InterestMake it relevant to the reader
DesireShow the benefits, remove doubts
ActionAsk for a clear, specific response

Match the scenario

ScenarioStrategy
Request missed lecture slidesDirect
3-day extension on a final projectProblem-solving
Free trial of a premium database toolSales pattern
Convince teacher to invite a guest speakerSales pattern
Request a new computer from ITProblem-solving
Confirm enrollment in a workshopDirect
Reconsider an unfair performance reviewProblem-solving

Model answer — a persuasive email

Scenario: Convince your teacher to invite a CS industry expert to speak to your class.

Subject: Request to Invite an Industry Expert for Our Class Dear Sir, After our recent class discussion on emerging technologies, I would like to suggest inviting a real-world industry professional to speak to our class. ← Attention Many of us found the topic exciting, but we feel that hearing from someone who works in the field would make the ideas much clearer and more real. ← Interest A short session with a software engineer or AI developer would give us true insight into how these technologies are used in industry, and could inspire many of us in choosing our careers. ← Desire Could we discuss arranging such a session? I would be happy to help coordinate and organize it. Thank you for considering this idea. ← Action Best regards, Muhammad Arham Rajput
💡 Tips & tricks
  • The trick to "match the strategy" questions: ask "will they easily say yes?" If yes → Direct. If they need convincing → Problem-solving. If you're selling something they don't want yet → Sales
  • For a sales-pattern answer, label your paragraphs A-I-D-A in your head so you don't miss a stage
  • Always end persuasive messages with a clear, specific action — never a vague "let me know"

Practice questions

Q1 What does AIDA stand for? Explain each stage in one line.
Attention — grab the reader's interest with a strong opening. Interest — build relevance so they keep reading. Desire — show the benefits and reduce objections. Action — ask for a clear, specific response.
Q2 Which strategy fits: asking your supervisor to reconsider an unfair review? Why?
Problem-solving. The reader may resist, so you open with a shared concern, build common ground, present your case calmly, and then ask for the review to be reconsidered.
Topic 4

Developing a user manual

A user guide (or manual) is a document that helps people use a product or system — for training, operating, servicing, or repairing it.

Two ways to write a manual

Feature-oriented
Explains each button, knob, and menu one by one
·
Task-oriented
Focuses on getting a job done, step by step

How to write clear instructions

Use imperative verbs
"Click", "Press", "Insert" — give a direct command
›
One action per step
Don't combine two actions in a single step
›
Logical order, max 8 steps
Follow the real sequence; keep it short

Fixing weak instructions very common

Wrong → Right
✗ "Allow the glue to dry adequately." ✓ Let the glue dry completely. ✗ "...quickly and in an easy manner rearrange headings." ✓ ...quickly and easily rearrange headings. ✗ "After having used the equipment, sliding the sensor back into its holder is highly recommended." ✓ After use, slide the temperature sensor back into its holder.

The fixes remove wordiness, passive voice, and indirect phrasing — and use plain command verbs.

Model answer — step-by-step instructions

Task: Write instructions to insert a picture in a PowerPoint slide.

Inserting a picture in PowerPoint
1. Open your presentation and select the slide. 2. Click the Insert tab. 3. Click Pictures. 4. Select This Device. 5. Find and select your image. 6. Click Insert. 7. Drag the corner handles to resize the picture. 8. Click outside the image to finish.

Each step starts with a command verb, has one action, and follows the real order — exactly the rules.

Parts of a user guide

Usually included
  • Title page & covers
  • Trademarks, disclaimers, warranties
  • Introduction / product description
  • Getting started & installation
  • Operating & maintenance instructions
  • Troubleshooting with safety notices
  • Glossary & index
Optional
  • Table of contents
  • Appendices
  • FAQ section
  • Reader-comment form
💡 Tips & tricks
  • Spot weak instructions by looking for: passive voice ("is recommended"), wordy phrases ("in an easy manner"), and non-command verbs
  • A feature is what the app has; a task is what the user does. "Has a search bar" = feature; "Search for a file" = task
  • Keep each step to one clear action — examiners deduct marks for combined steps

Practice questions

Q1 Rewrite: "Sharing of files between two phones can be done by turning on Bluetooth."
Turn on Bluetooth on both phones to share files. (Direct command, active voice, one action.)
Q2 Turn this feature into a user task: "The app has a dark mode setting."
Feature → Task: "Switch the app to dark mode."
Topic 5

How to read a research paper

Scientific papers follow a fixed structure. Knowing it helps you find information fast and judge whether the work is good.

The IMRD structure memorize

Introduction
Context & reason — why the study matters
›
Methods
How the research was done
›
Results
What was found (data, figures)
›
Discussion
What it means & why it matters

The Abstract sits before all of these — a quick summary of aim, method, findings, and conclusion. Read it first to decide if the paper is useful, but never rely on it alone.

The three reading tasks

1 · Comprehension
Understand the research problem and the contribution (what is new?)
›
2 · Evaluation
Judge if the problem is important, the contribution real, and the claims valid
›
3 · Synthesis
Generate new ideas: alternatives, counter-arguments, open problems

Reading the introduction — find four things

  • Context — background about the topic
  • Importance — why the issue matters
  • Research gap — what is missing or unsolved in past work
  • Purpose — what the authors aim to do

Model answer — analyzing an introduction

Using the paper "Agentic AI for Digital Twin" (one of your assignment papers):

Context — Digital twins are widely used in industries such as shipping, but they are complex and hard to scale. Importance — As data sources grow, combining them for real-time decisions becomes difficult, which limits how useful digital twins can be. Research gap — Existing systems struggle to bring together diverse, fast-changing data, and they are too complex for non-expert users. Purpose — To build an interactive agentic framework that uses large language models as a "universal interface" to pick the right tools and data automatically. Research problem statement: Digital twins in the shipping industry are hard to scale because of high complexity and the challenge of joining many real-time data sources. There is a need for a simpler way for different users to get insights. This study proposes an agentic AI framework that uses LLMs to manage tools and data on their own for real-time decisions.

Note-taking template

  • Citation — author, year, title, journal, pages
  • Keywords — general and specific subject
  • Hypothesis — what question did they ask?
  • Methodology — what experiments were done?
  • Results — key findings
  • Summary — in your own words
  • Significance — why it matters to the field and your work
💡 Tips & tricks
  • Remember the order with the word IMRD and the three tasks as C-E-S (Comprehension, Evaluation, Synthesis)
  • For a "research problem statement", use this shape: what the problem is → why it matters → what the study does about it
  • Paraphrase — explain ideas in your own words. Copying sentences loses marks

Practice questions

Q1 What does IMRD stand for, and what does each section do?
Introduction — gives context and the reason for the study. Methods — describes how the research was done. Results — presents the findings and data. Discussion — explains what the results mean and why they matter.
Q2 Name and briefly explain the three essential tasks of reading a paper.
Comprehension — understand what the paper says (problem + contribution). Evaluation — judge if the claims are important and valid. Synthesis — use the work to generate new research ideas.

Simple vocabulary

Your teacher prefers plain, simple wording over fancy words. Good technical writing is about being clear, not clever. Swap heavy words for simple ones.

Use this, not that

Useful words to know (with simple meanings)

  • Memo — a short message sent inside an organization
  • Cue words — the labels DATE, TO, FROM, SUBJECT
  • Persuasive — meant to convince someone
  • Objection — a reason the reader might say no
  • Imperative verb — a command word like "click" or "press"
  • Paraphrase — to say something in your own words
  • Research gap — a missing or unsolved part of past research
  • Concise — short and to the point
  • Coherent — clear and well-connected
  • Encs — short for "enclosures", documents attached to a memo
💡 Tips & tricks
  • When unsure between two words, pick the shorter, simpler one
  • Read your sentence aloud — if it sounds like normal speech, it's clear enough
  • Avoid long sentences. One idea per sentence is easiest to mark
